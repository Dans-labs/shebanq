What is SHEBANQ?

System for HEBrew Text: ANnotations for Queries and Markup

This is a system for the study of the Hebrew Bible, produced by the ETCBC in cooperation with the German Bible Society and the Netherlands Bible Society.

It is powered by the ETCBC database, formerly known as WIVU which facilitates powerful syntactical queries. SHEBANQ lets you develop and share those queries with fellow researchers. Scholarly editions of the Bible usually dedicate space to a critical apparatus and various kinds of annotations. SHEBANQ introduces the idea of annotating the text with queries. They show up next to the text pages where the results are. By sharing your queries with others, you may find what you did not search for. Of course you can also add ordinary annotations!

Get started: read the text, view words, queries and notes, and move around

You can just read {{=A(T("The Text"), _href=URL('hebrew', 'text', vars=dict(book='Genesis', chapter=1, version='4', qget='v', wget='v')))}}. In the margin you find related queries, and you can follow them. You can also start with a query, such as {{=A(T("shibboleth"), _href=URL('hebrew', 'query', vars=dict(version='4', id=18, page=1)))}} (1 result) or a word such as the {{=A(T("definite article"), _href=URL('hebrew', 'word', vars=dict(version='4', id=3933, page=1)))}} (more than 30,000 occurrences). Do not forget to use the clickable chart, which shows you how your results are distributed over the text as a whole. You can even download the results as a csv file and use your spreadsheet program to make a nicer chart. Or find an other {{=A(T("shared query"), _href=URL('hebrew', 'queries'))}} that interests you. You switch back and forth from browsing passages to inspecting relevant queries by clicking on queries in the margin and verse labels in the text material. Another way of navigating is by means of the cross-references between similar passages, which are shown as the note set crossref. The data that underlies the text can be viewed by pressing the data button. All this and more is explained in the {{=A('help', _href=URL('default', 'help'))}}.

Next step: make your own queries and notes

You can contribute queries and notes yourself, but then you have to register/login. When writing a query, by filling in your institution and your project you provide other users with a bit of context. Precise, concise documentation is at your finger tips, e.g. the growing body of online documentation on readthedocs. Once you are logged in, you can enter manual notes right away. Go to {{=A(T('notes view'), _href=URL('hebrew', 'text', vars=dict(nget='v', chapter=1, book='Genesis', tp='txt_tb1', version=4, mr='m')))}}.

Advanced: share and publish your queries and notes

You can share publish your queries and notes, so that they show up in the margin of the chapters containing their results. In this way they become a valuable resource for fellow Hebrew readers. See for example this set of real-life, yet tutorial style queries by Oliver Glanz, Reinoud Oosting, and Martijn Naaijer. If you want to cite your shared query or note set in a publication, you can also publish it. Your query and its results on a particular version om the database will be frozen, so that others will see exactly the same results later on. When newer versions of the database arrive in SHEBANQ, you can run the same query on the new data. You can modify that version of your query and publish it separately from earlier versions.

Expert: engage in data analysis and disseminate your results

You can generate notes sets by means of software, possibly aided by laf-fabric and {{=A(T('bulk upload'), _href=URL('hebrew', 'notes'))}} them to SHEBANQ. See also the {{=A(T('sources page'), _href=URL('default', 'sources#usergenerated'))}}. Quality results are candidates for the Tools page, such as parallel passages and verbal valence, a note set generated from the verbal valence patterns as formulated by Janet Dyk.

Free software: use SHEBANQ as you like!

SHEBANQ is free software. The tools used to run the SHEBANQ website and to do the data preprocessing, and all the recipes are available online. Moreover, the data on which all is based, is Open Access and can be obtained from DANS. You can reproduce SHEBANQ and make it better, or write totally different applications for processing the ETCBC data. Consult the menu item {{=A(T("Help/Sources"), _href=URL('default', 'sources'))}}. The license is such that nothing stands in your way, as long as you stay non-commercial.
