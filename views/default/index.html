{{=T('Get started')}}

Just have a look at the shibboleth query (1 result) or the definite article query (more than 10,000 results) or find an other public query that interests you.
Try to click on everything that has a color (except yellow)! In this way you switch back and forth from browsing passages to inspecting relevant queries.

{{=T('Next step: make your own queries')}}

You can contribute queries yourself, but then you have to register/login. By filling in your institution and your project you provide other users with a bit of context.
Start with the menu item My Queries. You can add a query by means of the + button, and you can edit, save, execute existing queries.
Precise, concise documentation is at your finger tips: {{=A('MQL quick reference guide', _target='_blank',_href=URL('static', 'docs/MQL-QuickRef.pdf'))}} and {{=A('ETCBC4 transcription table', _target='_blank',_href=URL('static', 'docs/ETCBC4-transcription.pdf'))}}.

{{=T('Expert step: publish your queries')}}

You can publish your queries, by pressing the Publish button where you edit the query (you can also unpublish them by pressing Make Private).
When you press Publish the query will be executed first, in order to make sure that the query results match the query instruction.
Published queries show up under Public Queries. They also are shown in the margin when you browse Bible chapters containing their results.
In this way they are become a valuable resource for fellow Hebrew readers. See for example the P"Alef verbs (by Oliver Glanz).

{{=T('About')}}

The shebanq service is the first data service of ancient-data.org.
Shebanq is a search engine for the Hebrew Bible, powered by the ETCBC4 linguistic database, formerly know as WIVU.
The data is archived for open access, clicking the DANS logo above will bring you to the treasure trove.
You can read about the history leading to this application by clicking on SHEBANQ in the menu bar.
Shebanq is open source. It consists of a webservice shemdros and a web application shebanq.

SHEBANQ goes live, but it is a demonstrator, with open issues. Currently we are aware of:
  1. The query engine might cause exceptions that are not yet handled
  2. The rendering of the spacing between words is not completely correct. Browsers are still not quite up to rendering classical Hebrew very well. Firefox does a better job than the Webkit browsers Chrome and Safari. We have tried very hard to work around the issues, so that you can enjoy the otherwise beautiful typography even on your iPad.
